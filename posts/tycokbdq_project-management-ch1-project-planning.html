[Project Management] Ch1. Project Planning

By Steven,

SDLC phases

While the specific number of phases can vary based on the model, most SDLCs follow these core stages:

  1. Planning and requirement analysis: Project managers and stakeholders define the project's goals, scope, and technical and business requirements. This stage often produces a Software Requirement Specification (SRS) document.
  2. Design: Software architects and designers create a blueprint of the software's architecture, including the user interface, system components, security measures, and database design.
  3. Implementation or coding: Developers write the source code based on the design specifications and established coding standards.
  4. Testing: The software is systematically tested for defects, bugs, and performance issues to ensure it meets the requirements defined in the planning phase.
  5. Deployment: The finished software is released into the production environment where end-users can access it. This may involve training users and providing installation instructions.
  6. Maintenance: After deployment, the software is monitored and supported. This phase includes fixing bugs, releasing updates, and addressing new requirements or issues that arise.

Common SDLC models

Different SDLC models approach the development phases in various ways depending on the project's needs.

  • Waterfall: A linear, sequential model where each phase must be completed before the next one begins. It is easy to manage but lacks flexibility.
  • Agile: An iterative and incremental approach that emphasizes flexibility, customer feedback, and collaboration. It breaks the project into short "sprints" to deliver incremental improvements.
  • Iterative: A cyclical model that focuses on repetition. An initial version is quickly built and then improved through successive, rapid versions based on feedback.
  • Spiral: A risk-driven model that combines elements of the Waterfall and iterative approaches, with a strong focus on risk analysis at every stage. It is ideal for large, complex projects.
  • V-shaped: An extension of the Waterfall model that runs verification and validation (testing) in parallel with each development phase. This ensures that issues are identified early.
  • DevOps: A methodology that integrates and automates the work of software development and IT operations teams. It focuses on continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD).

Benefits of using an SDLC

Following an SDLC provides significant advantages for software development teams and organizations:

  • Improved project planning and management: A structured process creates a clear roadmap with defined milestones, resources, and timelines.
  • Higher-quality software: Emphasizing rigorous testing throughout the cycle helps produce a reliable, robust product with fewer bugs.
  • Reduced costs and risks: By identifying and mitigating potential issues early, an SDLC helps prevent costly fixes and rework later in the process.
  • Increased productivity: Clearly defined roles, responsibilities, and deliverables prevent confusion and streamline workflows.
  • Enhanced stakeholder communication: The process promotes transparency and keeps all parties, including clients and developers, aligned with project goals.

1. Why are you interested in this job?

"I’m particularly interested in this position because it bridges both technology and education — two areas I’m genuinely passionate about. Over the years, I’ve worked in diverse IT and engineering environments, from robotics startups to large-scale enterprise systems. These experiences taught me the importance of applying technical knowledge to real-world problems, and I see this role as an opportunity to help students do exactly that — turning concepts into meaningful, industry-relevant projects."

I also value the chance to collaborate with academic and industry partners, guiding students through hands-on, interdisciplinary projects that integrate design thinking, technology, and business goals. This role allows me to share what I’ve learned while continuing to learn from others, which makes it very fulfilling to me.

2. Why should we give you an offer for this part-time job?

"I believe I’m a strong fit for this role because I combine both industry experience and practical technical knowledge across several of the key domains this job involves — IT, engineering, and design. I’ve developed and deployed software using technologies like Go, Vue.js, and Kubernetes, and I’ve worked closely with design and product teams to translate user needs into real solutions."

I also have project-based experience — managing deliverables, solving cross-functional challenges, and communicating progress effectively — which are essential skills for supervising student projects and ensuring collaboration success.

Moreover, I’m used to working both independently and within teams under tight schedules. I’m confident that my technical background, combined with my communication skills and mentoring mindset, will allow me to help students produce high-quality, industry-ready project outcomes.

3. Suppose there are several student projects, and some involve large organizations. How would you manage the student and client relationship?

"When managing projects that involve both students and large organizations, my approach focuses on clear communication, structured project management, and expectation alignment."

First, I’d begin by clarifying the project scope and deliverables with the client and making sure students understand the objectives, constraints, and expected outcomes. This sets a solid foundation and reduces later confusion.

Second, I’d establish regular progress checkpoints — for example, weekly or biweekly updates — where students present their progress and receive feedback. I’d facilitate these meetings to ensure discussions stay constructive and professional, helping students translate client feedback into actionable next steps.

Third, I’d serve as the bridge between students and clients — helping students communicate appropriately and ensuring the client’s expectations are realistic for an academic context. If issues arise (such as delays or scope changes), I’d step in early to realign both sides and find a practical solution.

Finally, I’d encourage students to maintain professional communication standards, including documentation, progress reports, and reflection. This not only supports smooth collaboration but also trains them in real-world project professionalism.

✅ Short spoken version (for live interview)

"I’d start by making sure both the client and students have a clear, shared understanding of the project goals and deliverables. Then I’d schedule regular progress reviews to keep everyone aligned."

My role would be to bridge both sides — guiding students to communicate professionally and helping clients understand the academic nature of the project. If any issues or delays happen, I’d address them early and facilitate transparent, constructive discussion to realign expectations.

The goal is to ensure students learn from the process while clients feel confident in the project’s progress and quality.
